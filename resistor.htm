The Resistor

A resistor is a device that limits current in a circuit. The value of a resistor is measured in ohms (Ω).

The symbol for a resistor looks like this:

A resistor's value is indicated by its colored bands. Here is the color code:

Color Number
Black 0
Brown 1
Red 2
Orange 3
Yellow 4
Green 5
Blue 6
Violet 7
Gray 8
White 9

Now, here's a simple circuit that you'll be familiar with when you start working with electronics. Using resistors with LEDs.

Here's an interactive Flash program that demonstrates what happens when you don't use a resistor on an LED, and when you do.

As demonstrated here, when the LED was connected directly to a 9V battery, it burned out. Never do this in real life! The LED could explode in your hand.

In the example with the 330Ω resistor, the LED was unharmed, and glowed brightly.

"How does this work?" you might ask. It's simple:

The LED needs 2V to light, and we are powering it from a 9V battery. Subtract the LED voltage from the source voltage to get your resistor voltage: 7V. Now, to figure out the resistance, use Ohm's Law... divide the resistor's voltage by the LED current of 20mA, or 0.02 A. The result is 350Ω. A 330Ω resistor will work just fine in the circuit.
