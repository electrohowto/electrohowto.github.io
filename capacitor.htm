The Capacitor

A capacitor stores a charge, almost like a battery, but weaker. Capacitance is measured in farads (F).

The symbol for a non-polarized capacitor looks like this:

The symbol for a polarized capacitor looks like this:

If you see this polarized version of the symbol in a schematic, the capacitor most likely has an electrolytic or tantalum-based dielectric.

Be sure to connect polarized capacitors the correct way or they'll explode!

Other capacitors, such as ceramic disc-type, can be connected either way. Just be sure to feed them a voltage at or under their limit; otherwise they'll explode.

Let's say we have a 100µF electrolytic capacitor rated for 16V...

  • If we use a 5V signal generator on our capacitor, we should be fine provided it has been connected the correct polarity.
  • If we use a 9V battery, we're still okay.
  • An A23 battery (12V) won't even harm the capacitor! But...
  • If we use two 9V batteries, we destroy our capacitor.

Capacitors can be used in many different ways, from altering the shape of a wave on the output of a 555 timer to smoothing out a DC power source for use with logic.

Capacitors can even be found paired with resistors in R-C low pass filters.
